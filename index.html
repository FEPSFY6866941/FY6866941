Abstract

The 2008 Great Recession marked a seismic shift in global economies, triggered by a financial crisis that began in the U.S. housing market and reverberated worldwide. This article examines the recession’s profound impact 
on economic structures, analyzing how financial systems adapted and reshaped policies to prevent future crises. By studying the recession's causes, government responses, and subsequent recovery paths, the research explores 
significant reforms in  financial regulation, monetary policy, and labor markets. Findings reveal that economies experienced a slow but resilient recovery, marked by increased financial safeguards and a stronger emphasis on 
sustainable growth. Concluding with lessons learned, the article underscores the importance of ongoing vigilance, flexible economic policies, and innovation in financial systems to safeguard against similar future 
disruptions.
